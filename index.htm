MAIN MENU



Measurements and Their Errors Covers SI units and their prefixes, limitations of experiment (random and systematic errors, precision vs accuracy, repeatability vs reproducability, resolution and uncertainty, and uncertainty in graphs.
Particles and Radiation Looks at the basics of particles (atomic structure, the strong nuclear force and particle emission, antiparticles, exchange particles and particle interactions, particle classification and quarks) and introduces electromagnetic radiation and quantum phenomena (the photoelectric effect, energy levels, florescent tubes, emission and absorption spectra, and how the theory of wave-particle duality came to be).
Waves Discusses the different types of wave (progressive vs stationary, transverse vs longitudinal) and their properties (superposition and coherence, diffraction and diffraction gratings, two-source interference, and refraction).
Mechanics and Materials Basics of mechanics including motion and acceleration, scalars vs vectors, projectiles, moments, momentum and impulse, work and power, conservation of energy and efficiency, and graphs of motion. We also look at the properties of materials and how we compare and describe them through deformation (density, Hooke's law, stress-strain and force-extension graphs, and Young's modulus).
Electricity The basics, IV graphs, resistivity, circuits, potential dividers, the electromotive force (EMF), and internal resistance.
Further Mechanics Dive into the world of circular motion, simple harmonic motion (SHM) including pendulum systems and mass-spring systems, different vibrations, and resonance.
Thermal Physics Thermal energy transfer and what causes change in internal energy, ideal gases laws (Boyle's, Charles', and the pressure one), and the molecular kinetic energy model.
Fields and Their Consequences A whole bulk of stuff in here... Goes over gravitational and electric fields, capacitors (charging and discharging), and magnetic fields (electromagnetic induction, induction laws-Faraday's and Lenz's, alternators, and transformers.
Nuclear Physics Looks at Rutherford scattering and the estimation of the nuclear radius and density over the years, radioactive emissions and the uses of emitted radiation (alpha, beta, and gamma), investigations of radioactive emission using Geiger counters, the exponential law of decay (the decay constant and half-life), nuclear decay (line of stability and energy level diagrams), nuclear fission and fusion (nuclear reactors and waste), mass defect, and binding energy.
(Option D) Turning Points in Physics Experiments and monumentous moments in the history of physics! How theories came about and were disproven and evolved: specific charge of the electron (cathode rays, thermionic emission, the fine beam tube, and Thomson's crossed fields); Millikan's oil drop experiment where he used Stoke's law (viscous drag force) and knowledge of gravity and the electric force on a charged particle in an electric field to discover that charge is quantised and work out the fundamental charge (the charge of an electron); the battle between if light was a wave or a particle through how Newton's corpuscular theory took years to be disproven and the development of Huygen's wave theory with the help of Young's double slit experiment and the discovery of Maxwell's predicted EM waves thanks to Hertz's sparks and Fizeau's cogs; the photoelectric effect and the photon model, revisiting the general idea and delving into the original predictions using classical physics and how that caused the 'ultraviolet catastrophe' and how Planck's 'quanta' interpretation of EM waves developed into Einstein's photon model of light which was able to explain this phenomena; the concept of wave-particle duality being introduced by De Broglie and how electron diffraction supported this, it also explains to us why electron microscopes work at such high resolutions, the speed of light and relativity where the Michelson-Morley experiment disproved the idea of absolute motion and ether using an interferometer, and finally how things that are moving at relativistic speeds (close to the speed of light) experience some very strange effects, how Einstein has tried to explain this with his theory of special relativity (inertial frames, time dilation, muon decay, length contraction, relativistic mass and energy) and how Bertozzi provided experimental evidence for the increase in mass of objects with speed.
Practical Skills Notes on practicals that you should be able to recall and describe.
