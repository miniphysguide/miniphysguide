USES OF SI UNITS AND THEIR PREFIXES


This page describes what SI units are and how to derive them. It also gives a list of common prefixes, what they mean, and how to convert between different units.


SI units

What are SI units?

SI base units are the fundamental units of which all other SI units (SI derived units) can be derived. S.I. is an abbreviation of "Système Internationale" because our metric system of measurements is an internationally standardised system.

Here are the 7 SI base units:    kg, m, s, mol, A, K, and cd.

  • kg (kilograms) for mass
  • m (metres) for length
  • s (seconds) for time
  • mol (moles) for amount of substance
  • A (amperes) for electric current
  • K (kelvin) for temperature
  • *and cd (candela) for luminous intensity

Note: *Students are not required to know about candela (cd), the unit of luminous intensity, for AQA A level physics.


Derivation of SI units

Usually when people mention deriving SI units, they are referring to deriving SI base units from other SI units. This can be done through dimensional analysis, which is just using known equations to 'calculate' with dimensions.


In dimensional analysis, each factor has it's own 'dimension' but in general terms (and to simplify things), you can just calculate with the units instead of the dimensions and it's a similar kind of concept.

For addition/subtraction, the units must be the same (or the same 'type' because you can just convert like how 1km is just 1000m). The outcome is just the same units.

e.g. Derive the units of change in velocity in SI (base) units when given that final velocity and initial velocity are both in ms-1:

change in velocity = final velocity - initial velocity

Both final velocity and initial velocity are in ms-1, so we know that the units for change in velocity have to be ms-1 as well:

change in velocity (ms-1) = final velocity (ms-1) - initial velocity (ms-1)
and looking just at the units... ms-1 = ms-1 - ms-1

...which makes sense if you make up an example like
2 ms-1 - 0.5 ms-1 = 1.5 ms-1

For multiplication/division, treat each unit a letter in algebra (as if each SI base unit is a variable).

Note: If that doesn't make sense, here's a crash course in algebra:

Algebra is just doing calculations with letters in place of numbers (we use it to work things out without knowing what the numbers are going to be yet).

The main things to know to apply it to units are that if two letters are next to each other, a multiplication symbol is implied between them
e.g.   a × b = ab

and basic knowledge of how indices work.
e.g.1.  a3 = a × a × a
e.g.2.  a-2 = 1⁄a2 = 1 ÷ a then ÷ a again
e.g.3.  a0 = 1 so b1a0 = b
e.g.4.  a5 × a-3 = a5 + (-3) = a2

That's all you need for this but as a bonus for just learning so well, when you add/ subract in algebra, the result is 'in terms' of the letters. For adding the same letter, we look at it in terms of multiplication
e.g.   a + a = 2 × a = 2a

so for a whole jumble of adding algebraically, we just collect the terms (terms are the groups seperated by + and - ) like this:
2s + b + sb + s - 3sb = 3s + b - 2sb


e.g Derive the unit of force in SI (base) units:

We know from prior knowledge that

force (N) = mass (kg) × acceleration (ms-1)

BUT newtons are not part of the 7 SI base units so it's an SI derived unit and not what we are looking for. We can work out what Newtons are equivalent to by looking at the units of this equation:

N = kg × ms-1

This means that we can rewrite N as kgms-1 so the unit of force in SI (base) units is kgms-1.

Now you can derive pretty much anything in SI (base) units!


Standard form

We can rewrite numbers in standard form to make them easier to use in calculations and easier to write down in general.

A number in standard form would look something like this:

                                              a × 10n

where 1 ≤ a < 10 and n is an integer.

For example,
27070000 in standard form would be 2.707 × 107 ,
and similarly, 0.000284 in standard form would be 2.84 × 10-4.

Pretty nifty!


SI prefixes

Using prefixes is another way to make really big numbers or really small numbers more bearable in calculations.

One that you should've come across before is 'kilo', like in kilograms.

Kilo means × 103 and this is why the following is true:
1 kg (kilogram) = 1 × 103 g (grams) = 1000 g (grams).

You can apply this to any set of units!
For example, a kilowatt would be 1000 watts etc.

But there's a whole ton of different prefixes out there as well as kilo to make life that little bit easier! The ones listed below are the one's you'll need for A level (and in general, to be honest):

Converting between units

To convert between units of the same quantity (so for example, converting between units of time would be hours to seconds or days to weeks etc.), you need to figure out what the ratio between the two units are.

e.g. Convert 5.96 × 10-11 J (joules) into MeV (megaelectronvolts)        to 3 s.f:

You should know that 1eV = 1.6 × 10-19 J,

by using this ratio, you can convert the Joules into Electronvolts.

5.96 × 10-11 J =  5.96 × 10-11⁄1.6 × 10-19  × 1 eV
                             = 3.725 × 108 eV

And then to Megaelectronvolts...

                             = 3.725 × 102 MeV
                             ≈ 373 MeV

so 5.96 × 10-11 J is equal to 373 MeV (3 s.f).


The same method can be applied to other equivalent quantities!

e.g. Convert 56 kWh (kilowatthours) into J (joules) to 3 s.f:

Since Power (W) = Energy (J) ⁄ Time (s),

we can figure out that 1 J is equivalent to 1 Ws
so converting into J is the same as converting into Ws.

It doesn't really matter what order you this in but we'll convert kWh to Wh first this time:

56 kWh = 56000 Wh

then, because we know that 1 h = 3600 s, we can convert Wh into Ws:

56000 Wh =  56000⁄1  × 3600 Ws
                     = 2.016 × 108 Ws
                     = 2.016 × 108 J
                     ≈ 2.02 × 108 J

so 56 kWh is equal to 2.02 × 108 J (3 s.f).

Note: s.f stands for significant figures, this means that the number is rounded. Rounding to 3 s.f means that only the first 3 digits are significant and the rest are 0s: if the digit after the 3rd digit ( the 4th digit) is greater than or equal to 5, then the 3rd digit is increased by one, otherwise, it remains the same.

e.g.1. 28793 to 2 s.f is 29000         (rounds up)
e.g.2. 73.995 to 4 s.f is 74.00         (rounds up)
e.g.3. 5.8232682 to 3 s.f is 5.82   (rounds down)
